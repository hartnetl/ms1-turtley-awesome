General Info

Sea turtles are a very important member of a complex eco-system. They're not only important for maintaining the eco-system's health, but they benefit local human communities as a source of eco-tourism, as many people (such as you, reader) are curious about these pre-historic animals.
Only 1 in every 1000 hatchlings will make it to adulthood. Sea turtles spend all their lives in the water, except for females who go onto beaches to lay nests, and the rare exceptions of those that sunbathe or get dragged onto the shore on the back of a female. These turtles face low odds of surviving to adulthood, and we need to do all we can to help them do this.
Despite being protected sea turtles face many dangers. Hunting, poaching and over-exploitation of nests are real threats to all species of turtle. The Hawksbill in particular is vulnerable to hunting as their shell is used in tortoiseshell trade. Turtles are vulnerable to getting caught in fishing nets and lines, and even if released they may be too injured to survive. Coastal development is making nesting beaches unsuitable. Turtles can be discouraged from nesting, more people and dogs can cause nests to be disturbed more frequently and bright lights can disorientate the hatchlings who come out at night. Species like the Kemp's ridley only nest in a small few locations, so losing even one beach could cause a rapid decline in populations. Plastic pollution is a serious threat to sea turtles. Plastic causes many problems for turtles, and when it's floating in the water it's often mistaken for food causing turtles to choke. Climate change could topple the stability of populations as sex is determined by the temperature of the nest. These dangers only exist because of people, so it's up to us to now undo some of that damage.

IUCN redlist classifications

NE - Not Evaluated. No data to tell the status of the species.
DD - Data deficient. Data about abundance/distribution is insufficient to assign a classification.
LC - Least concern. The populations are healthy and thriving without intervention.
NT - Not threatened. The species is doing well enough, but nearing threshold of being at risk of extinction.
VU - Vulnerable. The species is at risk of extinction from the wild.
EN - Endangered. The species is facing a very high risk of extinction from the wild.
CR - Critically endangered. The species is facing an extremely high risk of extinction from the wild.
EW - Extinct from the Wild. The species has no indivduals surviving in the wild.
EX - Extinct. There are no known individuals alive from this species.

Green sea turtle
(Chelonia mydas)


IUCN status:
Endangered
Location:
Global tropical distribution, but largest colonies off Eastern Australia and Costa Rica.
Distinguishing features:
These turtles have a thick layer of green coloured fat under their skin, which is where their name comes from. This most likely is caused by their herbivorous diet! They grow up to 4 feet long and weigh 140kg.
Fun fact:
These turtles often look like they're crying while they're nesting, but they're not sad (presumably). It's actually a way for them to excrete excess salt.

Loggerhead sea turtle
(Caretta caretta)


IUCN status:
Vulnerable
Location:
Temperate and tropical waters, with the two largest nesting areas on the SE coast of the US and the coast of Oman.
Distinguishing features:
Their big broad head. They grow up to 3 feet long and weigh up to 113kg.
Fun fact:
Their strong head and jaws allow them to eat a diet consisting largely of hard shelled creatures such as horseshoe crabs.

Leatherback sea turtle
(Dermochelys coriacea)


IUCN status:
Vulnerable (with criticaly endangered subpopulations)
Location:
This turtle has the widest range of all turtles, appearing in all but the polar oceans.
Distinguishing features:
They don't have a hard shell, but a softer leather-like one. They can grow over 6 feet long and are adorned with light spots.
Fun fact:
This is the largest of all sea turtles, and can weigh up to 900kg! They will travel across oceans to find their favourite jellyfish to eat.

Hawksbill sea turtle
(Eretmochelys imbricata)


IUCN status:
Critically endangered
Location:
These turtles choose specific reefs to spend their life, and can be found near coraf reefs worldwide.
Distinguishing features:
Their sharp pointed beak which allows them to eat sponges from coral reefs. They have a bright streaked yellow and brown shell. They grow up to 3 feet long and weigh up to 70kg.
Fun fact:
Much of the sponges and other food items chosen by a Hawksbill are poisonous, but they're able to safely store this toxin in their fat, making them in turn toxic to eat.
Ongoing projects:
Campeche Turtle Project

Olive Ridley sea turtle
(Lepidochelys olivacea)


IUCN status:
Vulnerable
Location:
Found globally across the tropical waters of the South Atlantic, Pacific and Indian Oceans.
Distinguishing features:
It's olive green coloured shell which it is named for. They are one of the smallest sea turtles, only growing to 2 and a half feet long and weighing up to 50kg.
Fun fact:
Turtles usually nest in isolation. Olive Ridley turtles may do this, or nest synchronously as part of a group in a phenomenon known as arribada.

Olive Ridley Project

Kemp's Ridley sea turtle
(Lepidochelys kempii)


IUCN status:
Critically endangered
Location:
The Gulf of Mexico and along the eastern coast of the US
Distinguishing features:
It has a very round, almost circular shell in contrast to the typical tear drop shape. They're the smallest sea turtle, growing just over 2 feet and weighing 45kg.
Fun fact:
95% of their nesting happens in Mexico at the same time (arribaba). This way of nesting increases the risk of a nest being disturbed while another is being built. They're the only sea turtle to commonly nest during the day.
Ongoing projects:
NOAA

Flatback sea turtle
(Natator depressus)


IUCN status:
Data Deficient
Location:
Only Australia and Papua New Guinea coastlines.
Distinguishing features:
As the name suggests, it has a much flatter shell than other turtle species.
Fun fact:
Despite having a strong carapace, they are predated upon by crocodiles during the nesting season! Australia also considers them to be vulnerable, but the IUCN classes them as DD.
Ongoing projects:
North West Shelf Flatback Turtle conservation Program
