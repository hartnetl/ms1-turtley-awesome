General Info

Sea turtles are a very important member of a complex eco-system. Some are considered the gardeners of the ocean, eating kelp and other plants preventing them from spreading too far. Others keep coral reefs healthy by maintaining levels of sponges. Most species are important to human populations as a source of eco-tourism, as most people (such as you, reader) are curious about these pre-historic animals.
Hatchling turtles emerge from a nest with their 100 or so siblings, and scramble towards the water. They face so many challenges at this young age that only 1 in every 1000 will make it to adulthood. Males spend all their lives in the water, with the rare exception of those that go onto the beach to sunbathe, or get dragged onto the shore on the back of a female. Females themselves only leave the water to build a nest 3-5 times per season. These fascinating creatures are a joy to observe, and it is up to us to clean our oceans to protect them.

NE - Not Evaluated. No data to tell the status of the species.
DD - Data deficient. Data about abundance/distribution is insufficient to assign a classification.
LC - Least concern. The populations are healthy and thriving without intervention.
NT - Not threatened. The species is doing well enough, but nearing threshold of being at risk of extinction.
VU - Vulnerable. The species is at risk of extinction from the wild.
EN - Endangered. The species is facing a very high risk of extinction from the wild.
CR - Critically endangered. The species is facing an extremely high risk of extinction from the wild.
EW - Extinct from the Wild. The species has no indivduals surviving in the wild.
EX - Extinct. There are no known individuals alive from this species.

Green sea turtle
(Chelonia mydas)


IUCN status: Endangered
Location: Warm waters across the globe, with the largest colonies off the Eastern coast of Australia and Costa Rica.
Distinguishing features: These turtles don't get their name from their skin or shell, but rather from a thick layer of green coloured fat under their skin. They grow up to 4 feet long and weigh 140kg.
Biggest threats: Hunting and poaching, over-exploitation of their eggs, loss of nesting beaches, climate change and accidental bycatch by the fishing industry.
Fun fact: They're the only herbivorous sea turtle during adulthood and it's thought this diet contributes to the green fat layer they have.
These turtles often look like they're crying while they're nesting, but they're not sad (presumably). It's actually a way for them to excrete excess salt!
Ongoing projects: SPOT

Loggerhead sea turtle
(Caretta caretta)


IUCN status: Vulnerable
Location: Spends a lot of their life drifting thousands of kilometers through temperate and tropical oceans. Two largest nesting areas for these turtles are the SE coast of the US and the coast of Oman. They might even share nesting beaches with other turtle species, like the Green sea turtle in Northern Cyprus.
Distinguishing features: Their big broad head. They grow up to 3 feet long and weigh up to 113kg.
Biggest threats: Coastal development, poaching and exploitation, and accidental fishery bycatch.
Fun fact: Their strong head and jaws allow them to eat a varied diet inaccessible to many other animals. This typically includes hard shelled creatures such as horseshoe crabs.
Ongoing projects: SPOT

Leatherback sea turtle
(Dermochelys coriacea)


IUCN status: Vulnerable (with criticaly endangered subpopulations)
Location: This turtle has the widest range of all turtles, appearing in all but the polar oceans. Now the largest nesting populations exist in Gabon (Africa), West Indes and Indonesia.
Distinguishing features: They don't have a hard shell, but a softer leather-like one. They can grow over 6 feet long. They are typically adorned with light spots.
Biggest threats: Egg exploitation and fishery bycatch. Their main diet is jellyfish which makes them particularly susceptible to plastic ingestion related deaths.
Fun fact: This is the largest of all sea turtles, and can weigh up to 900kg! They will travel across oceans to find their favourite jellyfish to eat.
Ongoing projects: The Leatherback Project

Hawksbill sea turtle
(Eretmochelys imbricata)


IUCN status: Critically endangered
Location: These turtles have a smaller range than others, choosing specific reefs to spend their life. They typically only leave to go to the nesting grounds where they were born. These coral reefs are found in tropical waters worldwide.
Distinguishing features: Their sharp pointed beak which allows them to eat sponges from coral reefs. They have a bright streaked yellow and brown shell. They grow up to 3 feet long and weigh up to 70kg.
Biggest threats: Illegal trade and bycatch. Hawksbill turtles are particularly sought after for their beautiful shells for tortoiseshell jewellery and ornaments.
They are also susceptible to entanglement in gillnets and fish hook capture.
Fun fact: Much of the sponges and other food items chosen by a Hawksbill are poisonous, but they're able to safely store this toxin in their fat, making them in turn toxic to eat.
Ongoing projects: Campeche Turtle Project

Olive Ridley sea turtle
(Lepidochelys olivacea)


IUCN status: Vulnerable
Location: Found globally across the tropical waters of the South Atlantic, Pacific and Indian Oceans.
Distinguishing features: It's olive green coloured shell - this is where it gets its name from. They are one of the smallest sea turtles, only growing to 2 and a half feet long and weighing up to 50kg.
Biggest threats: Coastal development. They nest only in a small number of beaches, so if these become disturbed or unsuitable to turtle nesting it could really damage the population. Nests are over exploited and they're still hunted for meat and skin.
Fun fact: Turtles usually nest in isolation, in their own time during the season. Olive Ridley turtles may do this, or nest synchronously as part of a group in a phenomenon known as arribada.
Ongoing projects: Olive Ridley Project

Kemp's Ridley sea turtle
(Lepidochelys kempii)


IUCN status: Critically endangered
Location: The Gulf of Mexico and along the eastern coast of the US
Distinguishing features: It has a very round, almost circular shell in contrast to the typical tear drop shape. They are the smallest sea turtle species. They can grow to just over 2 feet and weigh 45kg.
Biggest threats: This turtle is very susceptible to coastal development. Their nesting is concentrated in a singular area, which means if they lose this nesting space, or is becomes unsuitable, the population could easily suffer critical declines. On top of this, they're still largely under threat by predation, accidental bycatch and global warming.
Fun fact: Nearly 95% of nesting worldwide happens in Mexico, at the same time in the arribaba fashion. This way of nesting increases the risk of one turtle's nest being disturbed by another while building a nest. They're the only sea turtle to commonly nest during the day.
Ongoing projects: NOAA

Flatback sea turtle
Natator depressus


IUCN status: Data Deficient
Location: Only found around the Australian and Papua New Guinea coastlines.
Distinguishing features: As the name suggests, it's shell is much, much flatter than other turtle species. They can grow just over 3 feet long and weigh up to 90kg.
Biggest threats: Coastal development, over exploitation and coral reef loss. Australian coral reefs are under considerable stress, like other reefs all over the world, but the scarcity of this turtle emphasizes the need for their protection.
Fun fact: Despite having a strong carapace, they are predated upon by crocodiles during the nesting season! Australia also considers them to be vulnerable, but the IUCN classes them as DD. This is probably because of the relative remoteness of the species.
Ongoing projects: North West Shelf Flatback Turtle conservation Program
