General Info

Sea turtles are a very important member of a complex eco-system. Some are considered the gardeners of the ocean, eating kelp and other plants preventing them from spreading too far. Others keep coral reefs healthy by maintaining levels of sponges. Most species are important to human populations as a source of eco-tourism, as most people (such as you, reader) are curious about these pre-historic animals. Hatchling turtles emerge from a nest with there 100 or so siblings, and scramble towards the water. They face threats all around from the second they are alive. Nests are threatened by human poachers and wild animals. When they emerge from the nest there are many predtators - birds in the sky, foxes, coyotes and dogs on land, and many predators in the ocean. 1 in every 1000 will make it to adulthood. Males spend all their lives in the water, with the rare exception of those that go onto the beach to sunbathe, or get dragged onto the shore on the back of a female. Females themselves only leave the water to build a nest. She'll nest 3-5 times a season, laying 100 or so eggs each time. These fascinating creatures are a joy to observe, and it is up to us to fight for their protection.

Green sea turtle (Chelonia mydas)


IUCN status: Endangered
Location: Warm waters across the globe, with the largest colonies off the Eastern coast of Australia and Costa Rica
Distinguishing features: These turtles don't get their name from their skin or shell, but from their green coloured fat. Large, tear shaped shell. 4 feet long and weighs 140kg.
Biggest threats: Hunting, over exploitation of their eggs, loss of nesting beaches, climate change and accidental bycatch by the fishing industry
Fun fact: They're the only herbivorous sea turtle and it's thought their diet contributes to the green fat layer they have.
Ongoing projects: SPOT

Loggerhead sea turtle (Caretta caretta)


IUCN status: Vulnerable
Location: Spend a lot of their life drifting thousands of kilometers through temperate and tropical oceans. Two largest nesting areas for these turtles are the SE coast of the US and the coast of Oman.
Distinguising features: Their big broad head. They grow up to 3 feet long and weight up to 113kg.
Biggest threats: Coastal development and bycatch.
Fun fact: Their stong head and jaws allow them to eat a varied diet which includes horseshoe crabs and other hard shelled creatures.
Ongoing projects: SPOT

Leatherback sea turtle (Dermochelys coriacea)


IUCN status: Vulnerable (with criticaly endangered subpopulations)
Location: This turtle has the widest rangee of all turtles, appearing in all but the polar oceans. Now the largest nesting populations exist in Gabon (Africa), West Indes and Indonesia.
Distinguising features: They don't have a hard shell, but a softer leather-like one. They can grow over 6 feet long. They are typically adorned with light spots and they have 7 ridges running down their back.
Biggest threats: Egg exploitation and fishery bycatch. Their main diet is jellyfish which makes them particularly susceptible to plastic ingestion related death.
Fun fact: This is the largest of all sea turtles, and can weigh up to 500kg! They will travel across oceans to find their favourite jellyfish to eat.
Ongoing projects: The Leatherback Project

Hawksbill sea turtle (Eretmochelys imbricata)


IUCN status: Critically endangered
Location: These turtles have a smaller range than others, choosing specific reefs to spend their life. They typically only leave to go to the nesting grounds where they were born. Their coraf reefs are found in tropical opeans across over 100 countries worldwide.
Distinguising features: Their sharp pointed beak, which allows them to get sponges from coral reefs. They have a bright streaked yellow and brown shell. They grow up to 3 feet long and weigh up to 70kg.
Biggest threats: Illegal trade and bycatch. Hawksbill turtles are particularly sought after for their beautiful shells for tortoiseshell jewellery and ornaments.
They are also susceptible to entanglement in gillnets and fish hook capture.
Fun fact: Much of the sponges and other food items chosen by a Hawksbill are poisonous, but they're able to safely store this toxin in their fat, making them in turn toxic to eat.
Ongoing projects: Campeche Turtle Project

Olive Ridley sea turtle (Lepidochelys olivacea)


IUCN status: Vulnerable
Location: Found globally across the tropical waters of the South Atlantic, Pacific and Indian Oceans. They nest on only a few beaches and may do this as part of a group (arribada where they synchronously emerge on a beach to nest) or in the typical fashion of emerging one by one when they're ready within the nesting season.
Distinguising features: It's olive green coloured shell - this is where it gets its name from.
Biggest threats: Coastal development. They nest only in a small number of beaches, so if these become disturbed or unsuitable to turtle nesting it could really damage the population. Nests are over exploited and they're still hunted for meat and skin.
Fun fact: This is one of the smallest but most abundant of all sea turtles, Only grow to 2 and a half feet long and weight up to 50kg.
Ongoing projects: Olive Ridley Project

Kemp's Ridley sea turtle (Lepidochelys kempii)


IUCN status: Critically endangered
Location: The Gulf of Mexico and along the eastern coast of the US
Distinguishing features: It's shell is very round, almost circular. They are the smallest sea turtle. They can grow to just over 2 feet and weigh 45kg.
Biggest threats: Predation, bycatch and global warming.
Fun fact: Nearly 95% of nesting worldwide happens in Mexico, at the same time in the arribaba fashion. They're the only sea turtle to commonly nest during the day
Ongoing projects: NOAA
